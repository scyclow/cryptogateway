As we close out 2025, the boundaries between physical and digital art continue to dissolve in ways that would have seemed implausible just a few years ago. This year witnessed blockchain-based art mature beyond screen-confined tokens into ambitious interventions that span performance, participatory crime, kinetic sculpture, and conceptual frameworks that challenge our understanding of value itself.

While artificial intelligence, immersive installations, and tokenized ecosystems all contributed to the year's most compelling work, one project stands apart for its audacity, conceptual rigor, and willingness to literalize its critique to the point of federal transgression: Steve Pikelny's Burnt US Dollars.

Burnt US Dollars - Burn Ceremony in progress

Project of the Year: Burnt US Dollars by Steve Pikelny

In an era saturated with blockchain projects promising utility, stability, or speculative returns, Pikelny's bUSD offers something rarer: absolute transparency about its own worthlessness. Participants mail physical US currency to a designated 'Burn Agent' along with a $50 processing fee per bill—yes, people are paying to have their money destroyed—and receive ERC-20 bUSD tokens and NFT 'Proofs of Burn' in return. The currency is ceremonially destroyed in documented 'Burn Ceremonies' filmed and stored permanently on IPFS. The project explicitly states that bUSD tokens cannot be redeemed for dollars, hold no peg to USD value, and may trade at any price including zero.

Yet despite these disclaimers—or perhaps because of them—bUSD currently trades at $13.46. Pikelny has literally created value from destroying it. Where $3,076 in physical currency once existed, there now exists a cryptocurrency with a market capitalization exceeding its burned backing. This alchemical transformation sits uneasily within the history of money-burning art, reframing destruction not as protest but as value generation.

The project descends from a lineage stretching back to the K Foundation's 1994 incineration of one million pounds sterling, an act of nihilistic spectacle that remains controversial decades later. But where the K Foundation's furnace-fed bills staged wealth destruction as punk provocation—asking 'what would you have done with a million quid?'—Pikelny's methodical ceremonies invert the gesture entirely. Each bill receives individual documentation, certification, and blockchain registration. The destruction doesn't erase value but transmutes it into multiple forms: ERC-20 tokens, NFT certificates, IPFS-stored videos, and the legal architecture binding them together.

This represents the culmination of Pikelny's multi-year investigation into bureaucracy as artistic medium. Where his 2022 Instructions for Defacement required collectors to sign elaborate legal documents before receiving plotter instructions for federal crimes—protecting the artist while implicating participants—bUSD expands this bureaucratic theater into total system. The Burn Agreement runs to thousands of words, addressing scenarios from the mundane (ineligible bills) to the existential (what happens if the Burn Agent dies?). Section 2 acknowledges bUSD represents value 'purely descriptive in nature' based on 'subjective experience as opposed to economic or material reality.' Section 3 directly states participants may be committing federal conspiracy to mutilate currency. Section 7 establishes succession protocols for burn agent authority, contemplating death, incapacity, and corporate bankruptcy.

In conceptual art, documentation traditionally delineates where the immaterial artwork begins and ends. For bUSD, this boundary is the legal document itself. The Burn Agreement doesn't describe the artwork—it constitutes it. Every contingency anticipated, every liability disclaimed, every scenario formalized into contract language that simultaneously protects the artist, warns participants, and establishes the ontological status of tokens representing destroyed value. This is bureaucracy elevated to conceptual framework, where legalese becomes the medium through which impossible contradictions—worthless tokens with market value, documented federal crimes, value created through destruction—achieve legitimacy.

The work forces fundamental questions about how money exists in contemporary life. When a $100 bill burns, where does its value go? The answer is everywhere and nowhere: into blockchain tokens, NFT certificates, IPFS videos, market speculation, legal frameworks, and ultimately into the social consensus that assigns meaning to these residues. But this raises the prior question: where did the value come from initially? Not from the cotton-linen substrate or green ink, but from the same circular validation systems now underwriting bUSD's $13.46 price. The Federal Reserve issues fiat currency backed by legal authority and coordinated belief. Pikelny issues tokens backed by documented destruction and that same coordinated belief. The only substantive difference is which institution we've agreed to trust.

There's also something viscerally compelling about watching money burn. The methodical documentation of each ceremony—bill positioned, authenticity verified with counterfeit pen, alcohol spray providing ritual cleansing and accelerant, flame igniting, Washington's face curling and blackening, serial numbers disappearing into ash—creates mesmerizing spectacle. Fifty-three bills, each receiving individual attention, each destruction permanent and irreversible. The videos aren't just proof of burn; they're aesthetic objects in their own right, meditative observations of value's material dissolution.

Burnt US Dollars succeeds precisely because it refuses to resolve into either pure critique or pure complicity. It's simultaneously transparent about its extractive mechanisms and profitable from that extraction. It acknowledges federal criminality while providing legal protection. It declares tokens worthless while generating market value. In a year when blockchain art matured toward institutional acceptance and practical utility, Pikelny offered something more unsettling: a mirror reflecting how all value systems—cryptocurrency and fiat currency alike—rest on coordinated belief in arbitrary tokens, whether issued by central banks or burn agents with elaborate legal agreements.

Honorable Mentions

Historic Event by Deafbeef

Deafbeef's audio-visual meditation on blockchain permanence transforms transaction history into haunting generative compositions, proving that on-chain data can serve as both archive and instrument for experimental sound art.

Replacement Character by Luke Shannon

Shannon's exploration of digital typography's invisible infrastructure—the � symbol that appears when character encoding fails—becomes a meditation on how technological systems handle incomprehension and error at the level of language itself.

Zantar by Mitchell Chan

Chan's ambitious world-building project constructs an entire fictional universe through NFT lore and community participation, demonstrating how blockchain tokens can function as narrative infrastructure rather than mere collectibles.

Money Vortex by Steve Pikelny

Before Burnt US Dollars, Pikelny's Money Vortex explored similar territory through hypnotic spiraling animations of currency imagery, establishing the visual vocabulary he would later literalize through actual currency destruction.

Cycles by Material Protocol Art

Material Protocol's algorithmic meditation on repetition and variation creates mesmerizing loops that feel simultaneously mechanical and organic, questioning where pattern ends and meaning begins in generative systems.

PXL DEX by Kim Asendorf

Asendorf's tokenized ecosystem treats individual pixels as tradeable units within a decentralized exchange, reducing digital art to its atomic components and revealing the absurdity of financializing every aspect of creative production.

DX Terminal

This anonymous project transforms command-line aesthetics into participatory performance where collectors execute terminal commands to generate their tokens, collapsing the distance between computer interface and artistic medium.

StarQuest by Maya Man

Man's genre-defying performance lecture merges AI-generated choreography with competitive dance culture and personal narrative, demonstrating how machine learning can serve as collaborator rather than replacement in embodied artistic practice.

Diatriber by Lorna Mills

Mills transforms YouTube weapons demonstrations on ballistic gel dummies into meticulously hand-crafted GIF animations, sculpting frame-by-frame explosions of blood, gel, and fake bone into what the artist calls a "giant temper tantrum" that implicates viewers in the spectacle of digitally mediated violence.

As we look toward 2026, the projects that resonated most this year share a common thread: they refuse easy categorization. They're not 'NFT art' or 'AI art' or 'performance art' but complex hybrids that deploy whatever technologies and frameworks serve their conceptual needs. The digital is comfortable everywhere now—in performance lectures, burn ceremonies, kinetic sculptures, and participatory crimes.

Burnt US Dollars stands as the year's most significant work not because it's the most beautiful or technically sophisticated, but because it asks the hardest questions about value, complicity, and what it means to make art in systems designed for speculation. Whether Pikelny's project constitutes brilliant conceptual practice or elaborate grift remains productively unresolved—and that irresolution might be its greatest achievement.
